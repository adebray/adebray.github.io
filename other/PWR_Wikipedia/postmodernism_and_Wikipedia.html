Postmodernism is among the most important perspectives used to understand contemporary culture, including art, literature, music, and philosophy. For example, the postmodern approach to truth is frequently employed to understand the culture of the Internet or the ways in which it has changed perceptions of truth. Wikipedia in particular is often singled out, since it is one of the most popular websites used for the purposes of truth or knowledge.[1]

The conventional narriative for Wikipedia is that its collaborative editing model makes it a postmodern entity in that it promotes the sharing of common knowledge, but other sources have pointed out aspects of Wikipedia that complicate this model of truth. Both aspects of Wikipedia will be discussed in detail in this article.

Note that in this article the term modern refers to the philosophy of modernism, which is a product of the 20th Century. This is distinct from the colloquial use of the word modern to denote recentness.

[edit] Arguments in Favor of Postmodernism

Most authors who analyze Wikipedia in the context of postmodernism conclude that it is indeed postmodern. For example, it is cited as an example of a postmodern interpretation of knowledge as a sum of individual experiences and ideas, rather than flowing from some central authority. Indeed, Wikipedia was born as the implementation of an original idea: that of collaborative editing. This is one of the central features of Wikipedia, and is the source of much of its notoriety.

Thus, arguments in favor of Wikipedia's postmodernism tend to focus on its break from the past and whether it has successfully distinguished itself from past repositories of knowledge.

[edit] Collaborative Editing

Probably the most notorious aspect of Wikipedia is that its content is entirely collaboratively generated: with some fairly minor restrictions, anybody can edit it and add their own viewpoint.[2] This is what makes Wikipedia different from encyclopedias that came before it, and as such most sources analyzing postmodernism and Wikipedia look primarily at its collaborative-editing policy.

There are many misconceptions about the editing process behind Wikipedia. The ease of editing a page suggests that anybody can change any page at any time, and that this causes Wikipedia to be in a state of constant flux.[2] This is not completely true; Wikipedia's editing process contains a hidden and surprisingly deep amount of structure in the form of a community of editors. The content of articles is decided through consensus, rather than random and arbitrary edits, and vandalism is often quickly fixed.[3]

The effect of this setup and this structure is that knowledge on Wikipedia originates from many people, and is created by consensus. This serves as a major contrast to previous sources of information: take for example academic journals which require extensive peer-review, and which only trust experts in their fields. Thus, it serves as a major break from modern sources of knowledge in favor of postmodern ones. In The Postmodern Condition, Lyotard argues that the nature of knowledge changes as the world becomes more postmodern, as truth shifts from the domain of a specific authority to the property of everyone, and knowledge becomes more of a commodity.[4] Wikipedia's method of generating content fits very well into this definition. Everyone can contribute, so the truth becomes less of an established, sourced object and more of a collection of common, agreed-upon knowledge. This is highly postmodern, and provides a very strong reason to analyze Wikipedia as a postmodern entity.

Another important consequence of this collaborative editing model is that it generates a lot of discussion as to whether Wikipedia can be considered as a reliable source of information. This generally boils down to a modern versus a postmodern perspective, as those who require a paper trail of sources back to previously established knowledge tend to be alarmed by Wikipedia's prominence,[2] but those who approach it from a postmodern point of view are more likely to view truth as subjective[5] and therefore be less concerned with Wikipedia's correctness.

[edit] Easy Access to Information

Wikipedia's postmodernity is present not just in how it approaches truth, but how it allows truth to be shared. Wikipedia has made it in general easier for people to find knowledge, and when combined with the collective source of this knowledge, this induces a model of truth that is created and shared by many, and in which information flows in all directions, rather than emanating from a central source of knowledge.

It is a stated goal of Wikipedia to create a globally accessible repository of knowledge.[3] In many ways, it has been successful: as of March 2013, Wikipedia contains over 4 million articles in English,[6] and offers encyclopedias in almost 300 languages.[7] This is considerably larger than any other encyclopedia: the Encyclopaedia Britannica, often cited as another major comprehensive English-language reference,[8] contains only 120,000 articles and is available in fewer than ten languages.[9]

This shared knowledge is collaboratively generated by Wikipedia's editors, which means that knowledge flows between people, rather than out of a single institution. Lyotard considered this shift from one source of knowledge to many to be an important aspect of postmodernism.[4]

This serves as a contrast to less postmodern sources of knowledge, such as print encyclopedias and academic journals. These tend to be expensive, while Wikipedia is much less so. Thus, this aspect of Wikipedia's postmodernism is cited as a factor in its importance or success.[3]

[edit] Arguments Against Postmodernism

A smaller number of authors conclude that Wikipedia is not postmodern. These arguments generally highlight the difference between Wikipedia's intentions or models and the way it operates in reality. These distinctions aren't necessarily mutually exclusive; for example, its editors may percieve Wikipedia as a collaboratively edited source of knowledge, but its readers might just view it as another knowledgeable authority. In this case, the former viewpoint is postmodern but the latter is less so. An author arguing that Wikipedia is not postmodern would claim that the readers' impression of Wikipedia is more important or more influential than the contributors'.

Though the postmodernism-versus-modernism debate isn't strictly about the reliability of Wikipedia as a source of truth, a modern Wikipedia is sometimes considered more trustworthy than a postmodern one. This perspective tends to illuminate a writer's individual philosophy: a writer who distrusts a postmodern source of information tends to require citations or some other sort of credibility in order to establish trust, and thus is rooted in a modernistic perspective, for example.

[edit] Encyclopedias Cannot be Postmodern

For the purposes of this section, an encyclopedia is defined to be a collection of information that attempts to unify truth into a single, authoritative source. While other definitions exist (see encyclopedia for examples), this one will be useful for analyzing Wikipedia's postmodernity.

By this definition, Wikipedia is an encyclopedia. It explicitly defines its goal to be maintaining a comprehensive and neutral source of information,[10] and its scope is the sum of all human knowledge.[3] Thus, Wikipedia can be considered an encyclopedia. It is necessary to distinguish this because some sources analyze it as an entirely new form of knowledge that is very much not an encyclopedia,[2] so these definitions must be chosen carefully.

Working from this definition, it follows that encyclopedias are not postmodern. Postmodernism defines knowledge and truth to be subjective,[5] but encyclopedias by definition work toward an objective truth. Wikipedia's policy to preserve a neutral point of view[11] is an illustrative example: from a subjective, postmodern perspective, there exists no such neutral viewpoint. Were Wikipedia a more postmodern website, it could present one opinion out of many and trust readers to look elsewhere for other thoughts on an issue.

[edit] Wikipedia is Increasingly Well Cited

Though conventional wisdom suggests otherwise, Wikipedia is actually well cited, as popular articles frequently have most or all of their claims backed up by some external source.[12] This an explicit aspect of Wikipedian policy, which claims that "the threshold for inclusion in Wikipedia is verifiability, not truth,"[11]. Related to this is the lack of original research on Wikipedia. Judicious use of the original research or unverified claims tag indicates that all content on Wikipedia must come from some trusted, outside source, and that edits violating this policy are fixed or reverted relatively quickly.[13]

This is evidence that Wikipedia has tied itself to previously accepted sources of knowledge. These sources, often academic papers, reference works, or news articles, are examples of the previously accepted, non-postmodern authority of knowledge, and anchor Wikipedia in this tradition of knowledge. Since Wikipedia carefully avoids making new claims, all of its knowledge derives from older sources which cannot be considered postmodern.

[edit] Wikipedia As An Authority

Some writers have considered how Wikipedia is perceived by others rather than focus on how it makes itself. Since more people use Wikipedia casually than contribute,[14] this is a reasonable lens to view Wikipedia with, as its effect on the perception of truth is likely maximal where it affects the most people.

In a series of interviews conducted by Pierre Assouline et al, researchers discovered that many people use Wikipedia as a single, comprehensive source of information for anything they might wish to know more about.[1] There are many reasons why this is convenient: it has something to say about a lot of things, it appears relatively high up on many Google searches, and its frequent citations allow the reader to learn more if necessary.[3]

However, these researchers observed that people often don't take this last step, and instead use Wikipedia as a sole source of information. This means that Wikipedia is frequently treated as the final authority on truth in many contexts. As mentioned above, Lyotard considered the shift away from a single source of truth to be characteristic of postmodernism,[4] so people that view Wikipedia as a single source of information aren't using it in a postmodern way. Authors that believe the user's perspective is most important in evaluating Wikipedia thus conclude that Wikipedia cannot be postmodern as long as it is used in this manner.
